Triassic Period (251-201 million years ago)

The Triassic period marked the beginning of the age of dinosaurs. Following the Permian-Triassic extinction event, which wiped out nearly 95% of marine species and 70% of terrestrial vertebrate species, the Earth was ripe for new life forms to emerge.

Early dinosaurs evolved alongside other reptiles, and by the end of this period, they had become the dominant land animals. The climate during this time was generally hot and dry, with much of the land concentrated in the supercontinent Pangaea.

Notable dinosaurs from this period include:

  • Coelophysis: One of the earliest known dinosaurs, this small, agile predator was about 3 meters long.
  • Plateosaurus: An early example of a prosauropod, it was one of the first large plant-eating dinosaurs.
  • Eoraptor: A small, bipedal dinosaur that is considered one of the earliest dinosaurs ever discovered.

Jurassic Period (201-145 million years ago)

The Jurassic period saw a significant diversification of dinosaur species. The supercontinent Pangaea began to break apart, leading to the formation of new ecosystems and the evolution of new dinosaur species to fill these niches.

This era witnessed the rise of long-necked sauropods, which grew to enormous sizes, and the first appearance of birds. The climate was generally warmer and more humid than in the Triassic, with lush vegetation covering much of the land.

Some iconic dinosaurs from the Jurassic period include:

  • Brachiosaurus: A massive, long-necked herbivore that could reach heights of up to 16 meters.
  • Stegosaurus: Known for the distinctive plates along its back and spikes on its tail.
  • Allosaurus: A large carnivorous theropod, often considered the "lion" of the Jurassic period.
  • Archaeopteryx: Often called the "first bird," this small, feathered dinosaur provides a link between non-avian dinosaurs and modern birds.

Cretaceous Period (145-66 million years ago)

The Cretaceous period was the last and longest period of the dinosaur age. It was a time of continued continental breakup, with the Atlantic Ocean widening and South America separating from Africa. The climate was generally warm, with high sea levels creating numerous shallow inland seas.

This period saw the evolution of some of the most famous dinosaur species, as well as the diversification of flowering plants, which became the dominant form of vegetation by the end of the era.

Notable dinosaurs from the Cretaceous include:

  • Tyrannosaurus Rex: Perhaps the most famous dinosaur, this apex predator could grow up to 12 meters long.
  • Triceratops: A large herbivore known for its distinctive frill and three facial horns.
  • Velociraptor: A small, agile predator made famous by the "Jurassic Park" franchise, though in reality, it was much smaller than depicted in the films.
  • Ankylosaurus: A heavily armored herbivore with a club-like tail used for defense.
  • Spinosaurus: The largest known carnivorous dinosaur, with a distinctive sail-like structure on its back.

Mass Extinction (66 million years ago)

The age of dinosaurs came to an abrupt end about 66 million years ago with a mass extinction event known as the Cretaceous-Paleogene (K-Pg) extinction. This event wiped out approximately 75% of plant and animal species on Earth, including most dinosaurs.

The leading theory for this extinction is the impact of a massive asteroid or comet, which struck the Earth near the present-day Yucatan Peninsula in Mexico. This impact would have caused:

  • Global climate change, including a prolonged period of cooling
  • Massive tsunamis and widespread fires
  • A dust cloud that blocked sunlight, inhibiting photosynthesis
  • Acid rain from vaporized rock

While this event marked the end of most dinosaur species, it paved the way for the rise and diversification of mammals, eventually leading to the evolution of humans millions of years later.
